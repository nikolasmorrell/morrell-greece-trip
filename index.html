Athens & the Acropolis

Days 2-3, 5 • Birthplace of Democracy

The cradle of Western civilization. Athens has been continuously inhabited for over 3,400 years. The Acropolis rises 490 feet above the city, crowned by the Parthenon — built in 447 B.C. as a temple to the goddess Athena. You'll walk the same marble steps as Socrates and Plato. Below, the Agora was the heart of ancient Athenian public life, where democracy was born. The Panathinaikon Stadium, built entirely of white marble, hosted the first modern Olympic Games in 1896.

Don't miss the Erechtheion's Porch of the Caryatids — six maiden columns that have stood for 2,400 years. At the Tomb of the Unknown Soldier, watch the Evzones (presidential guards) in their traditional foustanella uniforms perform the ceremonial changing of the guard.

🏛️ Parthenon (447 B.C.) 🏅 1896 Olympic Stadium 📚 National Library 👮 Evzones Guards
⛩️

Ancient Corinth

Day 3 • Where St. Paul Preached

One of the great cities of antiquity. Ancient Corinth controlled trade between mainland Greece and the Peloponnese from the 8th century B.C. The Corinth Canal — 4 miles long but only 80 feet wide — was carved through solid rock to connect the Adriatic and Aegean seas. It's a jaw-dropping engineering feat.

The Temple of Apollo (550 B.C.) still stands with seven massive Doric columns. This is where the Apostle St. Paul lived for 18 months and wrote his famous letters to the Corinthians. The ancient agora (marketplace) and Roman baths reveal layers of civilization spanning over a thousand years.

⛩️ Corinth Canal 🏛️ Temple of Apollo ⛪️ St. Paul's Basilica 🏛️ Archaeological Museum
⛰️

Meteora

Days 4-5 • Monasteries in the Sky

The word Meteora means “suspended in air” — and that's exactly what you'll see. Massive sandstone pillars rise over 1,000 feet into the sky, topped by monasteries built in the 14th century by monks seeking isolation from the world. They hauled materials up in baskets and nets. Today, six monasteries remain active.

You'll visit two of the six Holy Monasteries — likely Mega Meteoron (the largest, founded 1340) and St. Stephen's (the most accessible, with stunning valley views). Before that, the Byzantine Church of the Assumption in Kalambaka dates to the 4th-5th century. End the day at a panoramic viewpoint for a sunset over the Pindos mountains that you'll never forget.

⛰️ 1,000 ft sandstone pillars ⛪️ 6 active monasteries 🌅 UNESCO World Heritage 🌇 Legendary sunsets
🏝️

Mykonos

Day 6 • 6-11 PM • The Island of the Winds

Iconic whitewashed buildings, narrow marble streets, and vibrant bougainvillea. Mykonos Town (Chora) is one of the most photographed places in Greece. The famous windmills of Kato Mili date to the 16th century and once ground grain from ships. Little Venice — a row of 18th-century houses built right at the water's edge — is magical at sunset.

According to myth, Mykonos was named after Apollo's grandson. The island was formed from the petrified bodies of giants slain by Hercules. With 5 hours on the island (6-11 PM), you'll catch it at its most beautiful — golden hour through twilight.

🌬️ Famous Windmills 🌇 Little Venice 🏙️ Chora Old Town 📷 Most photographed
🏛️

Kusadasi & Ephesus

Day 7 • 7 AM-1 PM • Turkey

A brief crossing into Turkey to visit one of the best-preserved ancient cities in the world. Ephesus was once the second-largest city in the Roman Empire (250,000 people). The Library of Celsus (117 A.D.) is an architectural wonder — its façade stands 55 feet tall. The Great Theatre seated 25,000 and is where St. Paul once preached.

Ephesus was home to the Temple of Artemis — one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. Walking its marble streets, you'll see ancient toilets, mosaic floors, and a sophisticated water system that rivals modern engineering. Shore excursions are available from the cruise.

🏛️ Library of Celsus 🎯 7 Wonders (Artemis Temple) 📚 Great Theatre (25,000 seats) 🚣 Roman marble streets
📘

Patmos

Day 7 • 4:30-9:30 PM • Island of the Apocalypse

One of the holiest islands in Christianity. It was here, in a small Cave of the Apocalypse, that St. John the Theologian received his visions and wrote the Book of Revelation around 95 A.D. The cave is now a chapel — you can see the rock shelf where he rested his head and the crack in the ceiling through which God's voice is said to have spoken.

Above it stands the fortress-like Monastery of St. John (1088 A.D.), which looks like a medieval castle and contains priceless manuscripts, icons, and frescoes. The island's main town, Chora, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site of immaculate white houses.

📘 Book of Revelation ⛪️ Cave of the Apocalypse 🏛️ Monastery (1088 A.D.) 🌅 UNESCO Heritage
⚔️

Rhodes

Day 8 • 7 AM-6 PM • Island of the Knights

With 11 full hours, Rhodes is your longest island stop. The Medieval Old Town is the largest inhabited medieval town in Europe — a UNESCO World Heritage Site enclosed by 2.5 miles of massive walls. Walk the Street of the Knights, one of the best-preserved medieval streets anywhere, leading to the Palace of the Grand Master.

The Colossus of Rhodes — another of the Seven Wonders — once straddled the harbor entrance (destroyed by earthquake in 226 B.C.). Today Mandraki Harbor is guarded by two bronze deer statues. The town blends Byzantine churches, Ottoman mosques, and Crusader architecture in a labyrinth of cobblestone alleys.

⚔️ Palace of Grand Master 🎯 7 Wonders (Colossus) 🏙️ Medieval Old Town 🕛 11 hours to explore
🐂

Crete

Day 9 • 7 AM-12 PM • Birthplace of Minoan Civilization

Greece's largest island and the cradle of Minoan civilization — Europe's first advanced society (2700-1450 B.C.). The Palace of Knossos, home of the mythical Minotaur's Labyrinth, was a four-story complex with 1,300 rooms, indoor plumbing, and the oldest throne in Europe.

Crete is where Zeus was born (hidden in the Dictaean Cave to protect him from his father Kronos). The Heraklion Archaeological Museum holds treasures that rival anything in Athens. In 5 hours, you can explore the vibrant harbor town or take a shore excursion to Knossos — one of the most important archaeological sites in the world.

🐂 Minotaur's Labyrinth 🏛️ Palace of Knossos ⚡ Birthplace of Zeus 🌅 Oldest civilization in Europe
🌝

Santorini

Day 9 • 4:30-9:30 PM • The Volcanic Jewel

Possibly the most stunning island in the world. Santorini is actually the rim of a volcanic caldera — the center of the island collapsed into the sea after a massive eruption around 1600 B.C. (one of the largest in human history, and possibly the origin of the Atlantis legend). The cliff-top villages of Oia and Fira cascade down 1,000-foot cliffs in a cascade of blue domes and white walls.

Your 4:30-9:30 PM timing is perfect — you'll be there for the famous Santorini sunset, widely considered the most beautiful in the world. The sky turns gold, pink, and purple over the caldera. Visit a rooftop taverna, taste local wines from volcanic soil, and soak in views you'll remember forever.

🌝 World's best sunset 🌋 Volcanic caldera 🍰 Volcanic wines 💡 Atlantis legend
🌴

Samos

Days 10-12 • Island of Pythagoras

The birthplace of the mathematician Pythagoras (yes, a² + b² = c²) and a lush, green island of forests, vineyards, and turquoise coves. Samos sits just over a mile from the Turkish coast and was once one of the wealthiest and most powerful islands in ancient Greece.

Your full-day guided tour includes the Temple of Hera (Heraion) — one of the largest temples ever attempted in ancient Greece (only one column still stands). The Tunnel of Efpalinus is an extraordinary engineering feat — a 3,400-foot aqueduct tunnel carved through a mountain from both sides in the 6th century B.C., meeting perfectly in the middle. You'll also visit the Monastery of Megali Panagia and the Pythagoreion Archaeological Museum. The town of Pythagoreion itself is a charming UNESCO World Heritage Site.

📐 Birthplace of Pythagoras 🏛️ Temple of Hera 🔧 Tunnel of Efpalinus 🌅 UNESCO Heritage
